Task 1¶

Demographic parity: P(Y' = 1 | A = Blue) / P(Y' = 1 | A = Red) = (65/100) / (1/2) = 130 / 100 = 1.3

Equal opportunity: P(Y' = 1 | A = Blue, Y = 1) / P(Y' = 1 | A = Red, Y = 1) = (60/80) / (1/2) = 120 / 80 = 1.5

Predictive rate parity: P(Y = 1 | A = Blue, Y' = 1) / P(Y = 1 | A = Red, Y' = 1) = (60/65) / (1/2) = 120 / 65 = 24 / 13 ~ 1.85

Task 2¶

Evaluating fairness coefficients for models trained to predict credit granting with age as a protected attribute¶

We consider a problem of predicting credit granting with age as a protected attribute. We train a linear model, log-linear model and a random forest model on full data and a linear model on the data with substracted the protected attribute.

We evaluate three fairness coefficients: Statistical parity, Equal opportunity and Predictive parity. We also apply ROC pivot method to mitigate the data bias. We compare changes in fairness metrics and fitting metrics (MSE and MAE) and analyze results.

Linear model fairness coefficients¶

We observe Statistical parity equal to 0.14, Equal opportunity equal to 0.56 and Predictive parity equal to 1.60.

Error of the model is equal to 0.07 both for MSE and MAE metric. The model seems to be not-perfectly fitted (predictions are in the [0, 1] interval, so 0.07 MAE and MSE are rather high) and strongly violates fairness conditions (espacially the value 0.14 is disturbing).

Log-linear model fairness coefficients¶

We observe Statistical parity equal to 0.32, Equal opportunity equal to 0.61 and Predictive parity equal to 1.01.

Error of the model is equal to 0.93 both for MSE and MAE metric. The model seems to have more fair outputs (in terms of fairness coefficients), but is terribly fitted - MSE and MAE equal to 0.93 are huge. It is possible that the model swaps classes and if we applied prediction 1-model_output would get much better results.

Random forest model fairness coefficients¶

We observe Statistical parity equal to 0.36, Equal opportunity equal to 0.76 and Predictive parity equal to 1.09.

Error of the model is equal to 0.06 for MSE and 0.11 for MAE metric. The model seems be fitted on very similar level as the linear model, but achieves much better fairness scores in all three metrices (even though not all of them satisfy the 4/5 rule).

Linear model applied ROC pivot technique fairness coefficients¶

We observe Statistical parity equal to 0.80, Equal opportunity equal to 1.91 and Predictive parity equal to 1.33.

The method seems to force bigger values of all fairness coefficients. It allows to obtain Statistical parity in a satisfting interval, but also pushes other coefficients above the limit).

Summary¶

We analyzed the three fairness coefficients (Statistical parity, Equal opportunity and Predictive parit) for different models (linear, log-linear, random forest).

We observed that linear model performed very poor in terms of the fairness coefficients. The log-linear model achieved better fairness coefficients, but was terribly fitted. The random forest model achieved the best result in terms of the fairness coefficients and similar to the linear model in terms of the fit.

We can conclude that the random forest model is the most reliable in this setup. Although it is worth mentioning that none of the models satisfied the 4/5 rule.

We also performed the ROC pivot method on the linear model. This approach resulted in increasing all fairness coefficients, which allowed to contain the Statistical parity coefficient in the 4/5 interval, but pushed all other metrics above the limit.
